Gabriela Maribel Saucedo

Myop stood motionless, gazing at the lifeless man on the ground. For an instant, she hoped he might still be alive. Filled with fear and compassion she ran back home to ask her parents and neighbors for help.

When they arrived, they gently explained that nothing more could be done for him. Overcome with sadness, Myop asked them to plant a walnut tree where the man lay surrounded by daisies to honor his memory.

As the years passed, the tree grew tall and strong and flowers bloomed each spring. For Myop, they became symbols not only of death, but also of memory, peace, and the enduring power of compassion.

Acosta Nerina

Myop's mind started to spin restlessly as a lot of questions flooded her thoughts: Who was this poor man? What had happened to him? Why was his head on the side with all of his teeth broken? A freezing chill went down her spine. Who could be capable of such brutality? Who could have done such a thing?

Everything around her seemed to blur. She felt queasy and weak, her stomach turned upside down.

She ran blindly back to her house, blinded by tears, and told her mother what she had found in the woods. They hugged each other tightly for a while, tears streaming down their faces, and then they decided to return to the woods together.

On their path, the mother started to tell her about the struggles that the Black community had had to face over time and how most of them were illegally murdered.

Once they reached the spot, her mother picked up all the loose flowers while Myop looked down at the man and at her small dark hands.

The flowers she had gathered no longer seemed bright and colourful as before; they now appeared frail, like everything alive.

Suddenly, her mother started to sing a song Myop had never heard before, but it soothed her soul. Right there, with an armful of flowers, while holding her mother's hand, she realized that she belonged to a greater family. A sense of connection flourished beyond her small world.
